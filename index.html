Introduction
The Long Count
Fifty years of American incarceration, mapped for the first time at the county level.

The United States incarcerates more people than any other country on earth. That sentence has appeared in newspapers for decades, usually attached to a single national number: two million people, give or take. The number is accurate. It is also almost meaningless.

Who gets incarcerated, and where, and for how long — those decisions are made almost entirely at the county level. Judges, prosecutors, and sheriffs, operating under local rules with local budgets, produce outcomes that vary so wildly across county lines that neighboring communities can have incarceration rates five times apart. The national figure averages all of that away.

The map to the left shows something different. It draws on the Vera Institute of Justice Incarceration Trends Dataset, assembled from Bureau of Justice Statistics records covering over 3,000 counties from 1970 through 2026. Each county's color reflects its jail rate — the number of people jailed per 100,000 residents. Darker means lower. Orange means higher.

What follows is what that data reveals.

Chapter 01 · The Boom
Forty Years of
Expansion

The map is now playing through American history. Watch the orange spread.

In 1970, most counties sit in the dark — low jail populations, sparse data. The country incarcerates roughly 160 people per 100,000. Then, over the next four decades, nearly every policy lever gets pulled in the same direction at once.

The 1973 Rockefeller Drug Laws in New York established mandatory minimums for drug offenses that dozens of states would copy. Reagan's 1986 Anti-Drug Abuse Act created the 100-to-1 sentencing disparity between crack and powder cocaine. The 1994 Violent Crime Control Act — the most expansive federal crime bill in American history — funded 100,000 new police officers and $9.7 billion in new prison construction.

By 2008 — the peak — the United States was incarcerating more than five times as many people per capita as it had in 1970. Since then, rates have declined. But the decline is uneven: some counties reformed decisively; others barely moved. The map makes that asymmetry visible in a way that national numbers cannot.

Chapter 02 · Race
A Gap That
Never Closed

The national boom did not land equally. In 1970, Black and white Americans were jailed at rates that, while already unequal, moved roughly in parallel. Then, through the 1980s, they diverged sharply — and stayed diverged.

Nationally in 2019, the average Black jail rate was roughly three to four times the white rate. But national averages conceal the counties where the disparity is far more severe. Holmes County, Mississippi — in the heart of the Mississippi Delta — sits at the extreme end of this spectrum.

Holmes County is majority-Black, majority rural, and majority poor. Its jail rate pattern is the product of decades of specific local decisions: how bail is set, which offenses get prosecuted, how public defenders are funded. The racial gap here is not random. It is constructed.

Chapter 03 · The State
Why State Averages
Lie

The map has zoomed to Maryland. It is an instructive example of what state-level data hides.

Maryland's statewide jail rate in 2019 sits near the national median. That sounds reassuring — and it tells you almost nothing. Hover over any county to see why.

The variation between Maryland's highest and lowest county jail rates in a single year is larger than the difference between the United States and most European countries. These counties operate under the same state laws. The disparity emerges from local prosecutorial priorities, bail practices, diversion program availability, and the economic incentives built into correctional employment.

If a Maryland policymaker looked only at the state aggregate, they would see no crisis. The aggregate hides every one of those disparities.

Chapter 04 · The County
Dorchester County,
Maryland

Dorchester County sits on Maryland's Eastern Shore — a rural, largely agricultural county of about 31,000 people, hemmed in by the Chesapeake Bay to the west and the Blackwater National Wildlife Refuge to the south. The median household income is roughly $45,000. It does not look like a place you'd expect to have one of the state's highest jail rates.

Dorchester County, MD · 2019
—
people jailed per 100,000 residents

That rate is not explained by crime statistics alone. Dorchester has fewer public defenders per capita than Baltimore, fewer diversion programs, and a jail that provides some of the county's most stable public-sector employment. Once a correctional system becomes a major employer, the incentive structure around it changes — elected officials become reluctant to shrink it, and prosecutors face less pressure to divert cases.

The chart to the left of the map, if you were in explorer mode, would show Dorchester's full fifty-year arc — a rise through the 1980s and 1990s, a plateau, and a gradual decline after 2010 that has not returned rates to their pre-escalation baseline.

Chapter 05 · Pretrial
Jailed Before
Any Verdict

Not everyone in a county jail has been convicted of anything. A growing share — now a majority in many counties — are people awaiting trial who could not afford bail. The expansion of pretrial detention is one of the least-discussed drivers of the incarceration boom, because it rarely shows up in prison statistics.

The Vera dataset tracks pretrial and sentenced populations separately. Nationally, the pretrial share of the jail population rose from roughly 40% in 1970 to over 65% by 2019. In some rural counties, it exceeds 80%.

This means the majority of the people shown on this map on any given day have not been found guilty of the offense that put them there. They are there because their county's bail system treats poverty as a flight risk. A defendant who can post $500 bail goes home; one who cannot stays jailed for weeks or months while their case moves through the system — often losing their job, housing, and custody of their children before any verdict is reached.

Chapter 06 · Rural
Concho County,
Texas

Concho County, Texas has a population of fewer than 3,000 people. At its peak, it had one of the highest jail rates in the United States — not because of elevated local crime, but because it operates a regional jail facility that houses inmates from surrounding counties under contract. The jail is, effectively, an industry.

Concho County, TX · Peak Jail Rate

per 100,000 residents

This pattern — rural counties building or expanding jails as economic development — is visible across Appalachia, the Great Plains, and the rural South. Beginning in the late 1980s and accelerating through the 1990s and 2000s, hundreds of rural communities voted to fund new jail construction on the theory that correctional facilities would anchor local economies the way factories once had. Some did, briefly. Most produced a generation of inflated incarceration without the promised economic benefits — and left communities with facilities too expensive to close and too politically entrenched to shrink.

The orange counties scattered across the map's rural interior are, in many cases, places where that bet was made and lost.

Now · Your Turn
Every County
Has a Story

The six findings above — the national boom, the racial divergence, the within-state variation, the rural surge, the pretrial expansion, the economic entrenchment of incarceration — are not anomalies. They repeat, in different forms, across every region of the country. Every state has its Dorchester Counties. Every county has a fifty-year arc that its state aggregate erases.
